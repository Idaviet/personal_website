Projects


  • Master's Thesis

    For my master’s thesis at the University of Oslo, I applied machine learning and dimensionality reduction techniques to immune repertoire datasets to identify structural and functional patterns relevant to antibody discovery. I used integrated gradient analysis to recover physicochemical patterns from deep neural network classifications, exploring how sequence data relates to structural configurations. This work contributed to the broader goal of improving computational antibody modeling and reducing reliance on resource-intensive experimental characterization.


  • cryoEM Preferred Particle Orientation Optimization

    During my research internship at the Cryo-EM Swedish National Facility at SciLifeLab, I worked on optimizing the Chameleon System to minimize preferential particle orientation and other air-water interface problems in cryo-EM grid preparation. These issues can significantly reduce the quality of structural data, so I developed and tested strategies across a variety of protein samples and preparation conditions. I ultimately established a standardized protocol for in-house and visiting researchers, improving grid preparation consistency and reducing troubleshooting time for future projects.


  • Honors History Thesis

    My history honors thesis at UC San Diego explored a peculiar paradox in Norse mythology: how did a society with rigidly defined gender roles end up with a patriarchal deity who frequently transgressed those very gender norms? Combining close textual analysis with historical & cultural research, I traced how Odin’s feminine characteristics, particularly his practice of seidr magic, were initially tolerated and even useful for early Christian missionaries seeking to draw parallels between Odin and the Christian God. However, as Christian hegemony took hold, those same traits became a liability, and later Christian authors reframed Odin’s gender transgressions as shameful.
